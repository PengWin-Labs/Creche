# Concept Digest — cowork-2cf82ca8
_Session: "Automate notes relay process" · 2026-05-06_

## Key Findings

- **Google Workspace org-level policy blocks third-party app folder writes, even for account owners.** the Workspace account can write to Drive root but not into any folder. Error: `403 "cannot add children"`. A personal Gmail account does not have this restriction — it successfully wrote relay files during testing.
- **Root cause is account type, not folder permissions.** Setting folders to "anyone with link" or "Editor" sharing does not fix the block. The restriction is an org-wide Drive SDK policy in Google Admin Console.

## Architectural Implications

- **The write-path must be authenticated as the account with the appropriate Drive permissions.** Connecting the Drive connector with the Workspace account will always fail for folder writes under current org policy. Reconnecting as the personal Gmail account is the fast fix.
- **Connector identity matters independently of OAuth scope.** OAuth scope determines what operations are theoretically permitted; org-level admin policy determines what actually executes. These can diverge silently — the connector appears correctly scoped but fails at runtime due to an org policy the user cannot see from the connector settings page.
- **The relay architecture has an account-boundary constraint.** Household tier (Workspace account) writing to the observer channel in Drive requires either (a) the connector authenticated as the personal Gmail account, or (b) a different backing store not subject to Workspace restrictions. Channel design must account for this when the relay is hardened.

## Open Questions

- Is the Drive SDK policy changeable via Google Admin Console? (Admin Console → Apps → Google Workspace → Drive and Docs → Drive SDK → third-party app access)
- Long-term: should the relay backing store move off Drive to something without org-policy restrictions (e.g., git repo, shared filesystem)?
